SVD & PCA

Low-rank spatial models versus a color-space rotation ahead of the DCT.

Singular value decomposition (SVD)

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) factorizes a 2D image channel into three matrices: U Σ Vᵀ, where the columns of U and V represent orthonormal spatial patterns, and Σ contains singular values that scale their importance. Intuitively, SVD expresses an image as a weighted sum of rank-1 outer products (basis images).

A key property for compression is that the singular values typically decay rapidly. By keeping only the top k singular values (and their corresponding vectors), we form a low-rank approximation of the image. This reduces storage from O(N²) to O(kN), while preserving most of the visual energy. The parameter k acts as a direct control over the rate-distortion tradeoff: smaller k yields higher compression but more loss.

Compared to block-based transforms like the DCT, SVD operates globally (or on large patches), which avoids blocking artifacts and often produces smooth, denoised reconstructions. However, computing SVD is significantly more expensive, and storing the factor matrices (U, Σ, Vᵀ) requires careful quantization and encoding to be competitive in a practical codec.

PCA color prefilter

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is used as a color-space transformation prior to spatial compression. Instead of encoding the RGB channels directly, we treat each pixel as a 3D vector and compute the principal components across the entire image. This produces a new orthogonal basis where the channels are decorrelated.

The first principal component captures the largest variance in the data and typically resembles a luminance-like channel, while the remaining components capture chrominance information. Because human vision is less sensitive to chroma detail, these secondary components can be more aggressively quantized without significant perceptual loss.

Mathematically, PCA computes a rotation matrix Vᵀ from the covariance of the RGB data. Each pixel is transformed into this new basis after mean-centering:

y = Vᵀ (x - μ)

where μ is the mean RGB vector. The transformed channels are then passed through the rest of the compression pipeline (DCT, quantization, entropy coding). At the decoder, the inverse transform reconstructs the original color space.
